Hi [name],

Your Technical Report #1 (arXiv:2502.14143) names commitment problems and multi-agent security as core risk factors, and the foundation's thesis is that the worst cooperation failures happen when agents have different objectives. That divergent-objective setting is exactly what I've been building a primitive for.

The protocol: one agent's conclusion becomes another's only as a test the second agent re-runs. Coordination stops depending on inherited trust, because the verdict comes from replaying a typed check rather than from anyone's attestation, and a self-grading agent can't certify itself to a counterparty who re-executes the check. Replayability is the commitment device: the binding is structural, the claim ships with the test that would refute it. Two write-ups, a protocol (Verifiable Knowledge) and the data structure under it (The Hypothesis Graph).

On a contamination-free, post-cutoff bug, externalizing the check carried a weaker model to a fix the strongest released model couldn't reach alone. Preregistered, regradeable, DOI-archived.

I'd frame this as a measurable cooperation-relevant capability: the rate at which mutually-distrusting agents reach correct coordination under replay-checkable claims, versus attestation-only ones. That lands in your two priority evaluation buckets. The boundary I can't draw yet, and would want your read on, is the class of guarantees a re-runnable test can't transmit, where the test itself inherits the prover's blind spot. I'm raising 9 to 12 months of independent funding to build the multi-agent replay layer out, which is where the cooperation claim meets a real trial and can fail.
