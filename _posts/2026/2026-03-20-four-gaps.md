MOSAIC ([Duetting et al., 2024](https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.05905)) is the best paper on LLM ad auctions I've found. A chatbot importance-samples over ad candidates using advertiser reward functions, with a KL penalty τ that controls response drift. The Rochet payment scheme is clean and the mechanism is strategyproof.

Four gaps sit between the mechanism and any market that would adopt it.

## 1. The chatbot selects the ad

MOSAIC importance-samples over candidates using advertiser reward functions. The chatbot becomes both the user's agent and the advertiser's agent. This dual agency is the exact problem that [killed Perplexity's ad business](/perplexity-was-right-to-kill-ads) in February 2026: $20K total revenue, ad sales head departed. Users stopped trusting the answers.

The conflict lives in *who runs the selection*. Externalizing the auction into a TEE enclave resolves this without a privacy leak.

On the chatbot's own infrastructure, an enclave runs the [scoring function](/power-diagrams-ad-auctions). The query embedding never leaves. Remote attestation proves what code executed, and the user gets a cryptographic guarantee that the answer was independent of advertising.

MOSAIC's mechanism is incentive-compatible for advertisers. It says nothing about who the user trusts to run it.

## 2. τ is not free

The KL penalty weight τ controls how much the response drifts toward ads. MOSAIC treats it as exogenous — a knob the platform turns. It isn't free.

I [swept the equivalent parameter](/the-price-of-relevance) in embedding-space auctions (the log base maps directly to τ) and the tradeoff is monotonic: higher relevance weight improves user outcomes at the cost of revenue, no free lunch. The curve is smooth enough to pick a point, but the paper doesn't model what constrains that choice.

User participation elasticity binds τ from below. If the platform sets τ too low, responses drift too far and users switch platforms. [Gomes (2014)](https://ideas.repec.org/a/bla/randje/v45y2014i2p248-272.html) formalized this for two-sided markets: the optimal quality weight is always above the pure revenue-maximizer's choice because the platform has to retain the demand side.

The viable region for τ may be narrow enough to collapse the revenue model entirely. Does endogenizing the user as an agent with a participation constraint change the feasibility region? The paper doesn't ask.

## 3. Neither party gets receipts

The advertiser pays for influence on the response and can't verify the output was actually steered. The user can't verify it wasn't. This ambiguity is where ad fraud lives — [$84 billion per year](https://www.juniperresearch.com/research/fintech-payments/fraud-identity/ad-fraud-lost-to-fraud/) by Juniper Research's estimate.

MOSAIC's payment rule is strategyproof: no advertiser benefits from misreporting their reward function. But strategyproofness only covers the bidding side. It doesn't address whether the mechanism *actually ran*. Did the platform apply the published τ? Did it use the advertiser's reward function or a modified one? Was the KL penalty quietly relaxed?

[Cryptographic attestation](/attested-attribution) at each step closes the verification gap. TEE infrastructure can prove the published mechanism executed with the declared parameters. That turns the payment rule from a promise into a receipt.

## 4. The auction has no bouncer

A dropshipper and a twenty-year plumber both submit reward functions. MOSAIC treats them identically. Strategyproofness optimizes the wrong margin — honest bidding by unvetted participants.

Trust requires a layer outside the mechanism: composite signals (payment history, customer attestations, licensing) indexed in a public graph with federated curation. [Proof of Trust](/proof-of-trust) describes how email solved this without a central authority. The same architecture applies to ad exchanges.

Without vetting, the mechanism is incentive-compatible for liars and plumbers alike. That's not a property anyone should want.

## The missing player

MOSAIC models three players: the platform, the advertisers, and the mechanism. It doesn't model the user.

The market is already running this experiment. Perplexity tried first-party ads, earned [$20K total](https://digiday.com/media/perplexitys-ad-business-hasnt-exactly-been-a-hit/), and [killed them](/perplexity-was-right-to-kill-ads). Anthropic [vowed never to run ads in Claude](https://web.archive.org/web/20260217041818/https://www.cnbc.com/2026/02/04/anthropic-no-ads-claude-chatbot-openai-chatgpt.html). OpenAI launched ads at [$60 CPM](https://web.archive.org/web/20260217041818/https://www.cnbc.com/2026/02/04/anthropic-no-ads-claude-chatbot-openai-chatgpt.html) against [$14 billion in projected inference costs](https://web.archive.org/web/20260227151900/https://finance.yahoo.com/news/openais-own-forecast-predicts-14-150445813.html), with closed targeting logic no one outside OpenAI can audit. [Switching costs are zero](/three-levers). If OpenAI pushes τ too low — too aggressive, too personal — users leave for the platform that doesn't steer its answers.

The user's participation is the binding constraint on every parameter in the system, and the mechanism doesn't account for it. A captive-user assumption is fine for a theory paper. For a market, the gaps are load-bearing.
