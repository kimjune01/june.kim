I started with a one-line rule for ethics: adopt a policy as if you'd prefer everyone else adopted it too. Thirteen turns of stress-testing later the rule had stopped being the interesting object. The interesting object was the environment that decides whether the rule does any work. This is the trace of that walk, and the discipline that ends it.

## The reframe, and why it isn't enough

The line is [Kant's categorical imperative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Categorical_imperative) in a game-theoretic coat. Kant got eviscerated for it, because "would I will this universal?" smuggles its answer in through the description. "Lie to murderers" universalizes badly; "lie to murderers hunting my friend" universalizes fine. Choose the description, choose the verdict.

The standard move that dodges this is to stop legislating. Don't ask whether the policy is *correct*. Ask whether it's a stance you can occupy stably given that you'd want it universal. The filter is self-applied, not imposed. That's closer to [Gauthier's constrained maximization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morals_by_Agreement) and to evolutionary stability than to Kant.

But the dodge is empty, and this is where I have to be honest about what the conversation actually got wrong. A stance you can stably occupy and would want universal describes plenty of monstrous things. Caste ethics is coherent. Honor cultures are coherent. A purity regime is internally consistent and universalizable under its own description. Self-equilibrium is not morality. It's just consistency, and consistency is cheap.

So the reframe doesn't rescue the rule. It relocates the whole problem onto one question: what stops you from picking the self-serving description? Kant had no answer. Neither does self-equilibrium. The answer isn't in the rule at all. It's in the environment.

## Clean feedback is the variable

What catches a self-serving description is feedback that costs you when you're wrong. An adversary is the cleanest case: a free verification service, because acting on your false signal hurts them directly, so they have every reason to expose it. Markets test product claims with competitors. Litigation tests narratives with an opponent who profits from puncturing them. Where the feedback is clean and hard to fake, you can't quietly redescribe "lie to murderers hunting my friend" into "lying is fine," because the redescription gets caught and paid for.

[Skyrms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brian_Skyrms) and [Binmore](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ken_Binmore) modeled moral norms as equilibria of signaling and bargaining games: path-dependent attractors, not deductions from first principles. That move is right and it's the fertile part. But what selects between attractors isn't a single "noise" dial, even though that's where my conversation with Opus spent most of its time. Two things matter, and they're separate axes:

- **Noise**: how much uncertainty sits in the signals and payoffs.
- **Feedback quality**: whether the environment punishes a false or self-serving move, regardless of ambient noise.

[Signaling beats trial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Signalling_theory) only when the signal is clean; an adversarial game keeps it clean even when noise is high. That's why the honest variable is feedback quality, of which low noise is one contributor and adversarial structure is another. Hiring shows the split. The parts that are genuinely adversarial (coding under observation, take-homes graded against tests) stay distributed and discretionary, because the signal there punishes fakery. The parts that aren't (culture fit, "potential") are exactly where centralized rubrics and algorithmic screening took over, because no adversary cleans that signal and discretion can't be trusted.

## The diagram

So the phase diagram has two axes, not one, and the live one is feedback quality.

Where feedback is clean, universalizable morality can actually run: self-serving descriptions get caught, distributed selection between stances works, and pluralism survives even under high noise. Where feedback degrades, the description-gaming problem returns with no referee, and societies buy coherence the only other way available: by fiat. A prescriptive framework, religious or ideological or authoritarian, supplies an equilibrium without needing anyone to discover it. Under degraded feedback that's a real advantage, because imposed coherence beats no coherence.

There's a trap in calling that a "solution," and it's the strongest objection to the whole picture. **Centralization is itself a feedback-destroyer.** A central authority that imposes coherence also suppresses the local signals that would tell it it's wrong, punishes dissent, and manufactures correlated error. This is [Hayek's knowledge problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Use_of_Knowledge_in_Society): the cure degrades the very variable that triggered it. Which is why consolidation, once bought, tends to persist past the conditions that justified it. The regime suppresses the feedback that would unseat it, and you get [hysteresis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hysteresis): a society that centralized under bad feedback stays centralized long after the feedback could have recovered, because it ate its own correction mechanism.

I won't claim this is yet a tested model. Noise, feedback quality, and centralization would all need operational definitions before "the historical record fits it" means anything, and the loose version (call feedback clean wherever you see pluralism) is unfalsifiable. What I'll claim is narrower: where feedback is clean and hard to fake, decentralized moral selection is viable; where it isn't, the pressure runs toward imposed coherence, and the imposition tends to be self-locking.

## The arrow, narrowed

Read the diagram over time and there's a tempting directional story. Writing, contracts, courts, double-entry bookkeeping, reputation systems, verifiable credentials: each is a feedback-improving technology. As feedback gets cleaner, the conditions for distributed moral selection improve, and you'd expect some convergence. That much I'll defend.

But I have to amputate the grand version. What plausibly converges under cleaner feedback is *procedural* morality: reciprocity, promise-keeping, evidentiary standards, anti-corruption norms, punishment of defection. These are the norms that clean feedback directly rewards. Convergence on *substantive* morality, what persons are owed, what counts as harm, what is sacred, is a separate and much larger claim that this argument does not earn. Low-noise societies often make substantive disagreement sharper, not softer, because participants understand the stakes better.

And convergence is conditional on mechanisms, not automatic. Clean feedback lets you *identify* a defector; it doesn't *punish* one. You still need reputation, exclusion, exit, retaliation. [Tit-for-tat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tit_for_tat) doesn't win because it's nice; it wins under specific conditions (repeated play, recognizability, low error, the right payoffs) and loses without them. A cooperative stance with no enforcement gets harvested by coordinated defection in any noise regime.

So the arrow, honestly stated: under improving feedback, procedural norms that reward clean dealing become competitively attractive where enforcement mechanisms exist. It bends toward, it does not march toward, and it reverses whenever feedback degrades, which technology does as readily as it improves it (writing also enabled propaganda; reputation systems also enabled gaming; AI cuts search cost and floods the channel with synthetic evidence at the same time). [Parfit's convergence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/On_What_Matters) of Kantian, contractualist, and consequentialist views might be three faces of the same procedural attractor. It is not a proof that history improves.

## The one obligation it grounds

The framework refuses to name the good. It will name one obligation, and exactly one: tell the truth.

Every feedback-based morality runs on signals that can be trusted. Lying is not one defection among many. It's the defection that attacks the verification layer itself. A thief takes a payoff inside one interaction; the feedback that catches thieves still works afterward. A liar corrupts the feedback. The cost isn't local to the lie; it's a tax on every other agent's ability to catch self-serving descriptions, which is the one thing the whole system depends on. Lying raises the noise floor for the population to lower it for yourself.

So truthtelling isn't a value you have to import from outside. It's the precondition for the rest of the structure to function at all, the agent-level dual of "keep feedback clean." Kant made lying the one absolute prohibition and couldn't say why without going in a circle. Here the why is mechanical: lie, and you eat the channel that makes universalizability checkable in the first place. The [murderer at the door](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/On_a_Supposed_Right_to_Lie_from_Philanthropy) is the genuinely hard case precisely because it pits one clean-feedback duty against another, and the framework predicts that tension rather than pretending to dissolve it. That's the only substantive obligation the argument earns: not a theory of the good, but a proof that the good, whatever it turns out to be, can only be found in an environment where the truth is told.

## The one prescription, and the bridge to it

Up to here the argument is descriptive: it says which moral regimes are viable under which feedback conditions. It does not say which is good. Winning is not moral authority, and a framework that quietly slid from "this is selected for" to "this is right" would be committing exactly the description-gaming the whole piece is about.

So let me make the bridge explicit. **I stop describing and start recommending.** The recommendation is not an ethics; it's an institutional design heuristic, and it follows from the descriptive part only if you already want better rules to be findable:

**Maintain contestable institutions that make better rules selectable. Keep feedback clean: competition among participants, winners propagated up and sideways, and no one allowed to suppress the signal that would unseat them.**

This is the structure of evolution, markets, working federalism, and peer review when it works. It's also exactly what captured regimes destroy: winners rewrite the rules to suppress the competition that produced them, and persist by rule-capture rather than by being right, until a technology opens a domain their captured rules don't cover. [Winners write rules; the old rules die one funeral at a time.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck%27s_principle) The heuristic is the standing defense against that: protect the contestability, because it's the only thing that keeps "better rule" a meaningful phrase.

A corollary I didn't expect, and the one place secrecy intuition inverts: where competitor search is fast, a winning policy has a short half-life regardless, so leaking it on your own timing beats having it discovered against you. Secrecy is a moat only where search is slow. Elsewhere it's a race condition dressed as a wall.

## Where it stops

The seductive next step is to formalize all of this. If each pipeline is a functor, a society is a [monad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monad_(category_theory)) of them; equilibria compose up and sideways; the whole thing wants to live in a [Markov category](https://arxiv.org/abs/1908.07021) over a time-varying graph, with agents running [abduction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abductive_reasoning) so hypotheses propagate as signals and convergence runs at graph-diameter speed. Humans and language models fall under the same axioms, because the axioms don't care what kind of thing an agent is. It's a clean story.

I am quarantining that paragraph on purpose, because it is the part most likely to fool you, and me. Every step in it is structural plausibility, not a theorem. The 2-categorical coherence laws are unchecked. The time-varying extension may carry an [obstruction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tropical_semiring) I've watched stall adjacent constructions. "Abduction" has no agreed formalization, and different ones give different convergence results. None of it is load-bearing for anything above; if you delete it, the descriptive claim and the design heuristic stand exactly as they did.

So the honest place to end is the rule that outranks the others: **nothing is proven until Lean compiles.** A framework that looks rigorous to a human and won't typecheck is philosophy wearing mathematics. The golden rule I opened with turns out to be the least important part of its own story. The rule was never the work. Clean feedback is the work, the institutions that protect it are the work, and the proof, when there is one, is the only thing that counts.
